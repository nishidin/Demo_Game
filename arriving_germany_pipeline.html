Game design

Game loop & mechanics

Arriving Germany is a narrative-driven serious game. Players learn German cultural norms through realistic simulations — not lectures. Each loop reinforces behaviour through experience, failure, and guided feedback.

Core game loop
1. Encounter scenario
→
2. Make choices
→
3. Receive feedback
→
4. Score updated
01
Multiple choice scenes
Realistic cultural dilemmas with 4 options. One correct answer, plausible wrong answers. Immediate feedback with cultural explanation.
02
NPC conversations
Live AI-powered dialogue. Player types freely, Claude responds in character. Score adjusts based on formality and cultural awareness shown.
03
Drag & drop mini-games
Physical interaction tasks — sorting recycling bins, bagging groceries, organising documents. Wrong placements trigger cultural explanations.
04
Tap mini-games
Navigate real interfaces — ticket machines, registration forms, supermarket self-checkout. Step-by-step decisions with real-world consequences.
05
Map exploration
City map with clickable locations. Players choose where to go next, unlocking scenes. Builds spatial understanding of German urban life.
06
AI adaptability report
Claude generates a personalised debrief at session end — strongest area, main challenge, one actionable cultural insight. Different every playthrough.
Adaptability scoring system
Rule compliance
Following explicit German rules — transport validation, recycling, quiet hours, registration deadlines
System navigation
Operating German systems — public transport, supermarkets, bureaucracy, healthcare
Social awareness
Reading social norms — formality levels, titles, queue behaviour, workplace etiquette

Presentation guide

Mentor — talk track

What to say, when to say it, and what to show. Aim for 15–20 minutes total.

0:00 – 1:30 Open with the human story

"A qualified nurse passes her German language test, lands in Frankfurt, and gets a €60 fine on day one — because nobody told her that buying and validating a train ticket are two completely separate steps."
Pause. Then: "That gap is what Arriving Germany closes. Not with a handbook. With a game."
Don't start with your name or the slide title. Start with that sentence. It sets the tone for everything.

1:30 – 3:00 The game loop (30 seconds)

Switch to the Game Loop tab and walk through it in one breath: "Players encounter a realistic scenario, make a choice, receive feedback, and their adaptability score updates. That's the entire loop."
Point to the 6 mechanic types: "We have multiple choice, live NPC conversations, drag-and-drop tasks, tap mini-games, map exploration, and an AI debrief at the end."

3:00 – 8:00 Live demo — let them play

Paste the game link in the chat: nishidin.github.io/Demo_Game/
"Open that link and try the landlord conversation scene — just type something to Herr Fischer."
While they play, narrate: "Notice the score bar updating in real time. The NPC responds to how formally you write. That's the cultural intelligence being measured."
Don't narrate the obvious. Let them experience it. Only speak when they pause or look confused.

8:00 – 11:00 Progress evidence

Switch to the Progress tab. "This is what Month 1 produced — a live game, an AI integration layer, an analytics dashboard design, and a full game design document."
Point to the analytics dashboard: "By Month 3, a migration institute coordinator will see this — a named profile, competency tags, and specific insights for each player they send through."

11:00 – 14:00 Timeline + pipeline

Switch to the 6-Month Timeline tab. Walk through each month in one sentence each.
Then switch to Pipeline: "For March and April specifically, here are the weekly sprints — each week has defined tasks and a milestone checkpoint."
"I'm treating this like a real production — weekly deliverables, not vague monthly goals."

14:00 – 15:00 Close

"The thing I want from this meeting is feedback on two things: the game loop design, and whether the AI features I've prioritised are the right ones."
Then stop talking. Invite questions. Don't fill silence.
For Mentor (10 min total): paste the pipeline link immediately, say "I'll walk you through the March–April sprints in 3 minutes", and stick to the Pipeline tab only.
